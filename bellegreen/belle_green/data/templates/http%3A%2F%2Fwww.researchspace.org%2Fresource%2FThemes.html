Themes

RACE AND ETHNICITY

Though Belle Greene never writes specifically about her family’s decision to pass as White, she does make numerous references to race and ethnicity in her letters to Berenson. In these brief and typically vague passages, she alludes to the fiction of her Portuguese ancestry; exoticizes herself as Abyssinian, Chinese, Arabic, South Asian, and Egyptian; and makes several complicated statements about her skin color.

THE MORGANS

Belle Greene’s career was shaped in important ways by the Morgan family. Junius Spencer Morgan, whom she met at Princeton, introduced her to J. Pierpont Morgan, who offered her a job as his private librarian. After Pierpont’s death, Belle Greene continued at the Library under his son Jack. She also knew other Morgan family members such as Anne Tracy Morgan (Pierpont’s daughter) and Jack’s children Junius Spencer Morgan III, Jane Norton Morgan Nichols, and Henry Sturgis Morgan. Greene’s letters to Berenson reveal the deep admiration and trust, as well as the occasional tension, at the heart of her relationship with the Morgan family.

LETTER-WRITING

Belle Greene often reflects on the process of letter-writing or talks about its physicality—not only the pens and paper she used, but also the circumstances in which she was writing (train, car, propped on a pillow, et al). She often bemoans the volume of unanswered mail sitting on her desk at work, while also complaining to Berenson about his request for exceptionally detailed letters. As one of the great letter-writers of the twentieth century, Belle Greene’s thoughts on correspondence are fascinating and revealing.

ACQUISITIONS OF BOOKS, ART, AND MANUSCRIPTS

In 1908, three years after she was hired, Belle Greene began making independent acquisitions for the Library’s collection with Morgan’s approval and trust. She would continue developing its holdings in several areas, especially medieval manuscripts, early printed books, and literary and historical manuscripts, until her retirement in 1948. Many of her letters to Berenson offer interesting details of these acquisitions, including provenance, date, and the circumstances of their donation or purchase.

MODERN ART

Though Belle Greene’s passion and expertise lay in medieval illuminated manuscripts, early printed books, and Persian and Islamic miniatures, she also had a small collection of modern art and knew many contemporary artists and writers. She attended the epochal 1913 Armory Show in New York and frequented Alfred Stieglitz’s Photo-Secession Gallery, known as 291. Modern art seems to have simultaneously attracted and repelled her, and much of what we know about her response to the subject can be found in her letters to Berenson.

RELATIONSHIP WITH BERNARD

Belle and Bernard’s relationship emerged initially from a shared love of art and culture, but soon blossomed into a sweeping romance that for many years stood the test of space and time. While describing their correspondence as a group of “love letters” is misleading, Belle and Bernard nonetheless expended a great deal of ink expressing their love for another. In her first letter to Berenson, Greene addresses him formally as “Mr. Berenson,” but her greetings soon evolve into various terms of endearment, including “beloved of my dreams” and “you Blessed Lamb of God,” as well as less flattering forms of address such as “Egoist,” “faithless one,” and “man so far away.” The romance had cooled by the 1920s but they remained correspondents until 1949.
